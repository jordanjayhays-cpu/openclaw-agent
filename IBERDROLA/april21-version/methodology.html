EV Fleet Forecast

The 2027 EV fleet is the demand baseline. We use a white-line model — a declining-CAGR trajectory anchored to historical DGT registration data.

Historical anchor

Source: DGT vehicle registrations 2015–2023 (datos.gob.es). Fleet grew from 56,327 (2020) to 143,611 (2023).

Initial CAGR
CAGR = (143,611 / 56,327)^(1/3) − 1 ≈ 36.6%
Declining growth model

Growth decays 15% per year (market maturation) and is capped at 50% of total new car sales. Fleet loses 1.5%/yr to attrition (end-of-life, export).

Year-on-year projection
growth_t = growth_{t-1} × (1 − 0.15)
fleet_t = fleet_{t-1} × (1 + growth_t) × (1 − 0.015)

Station Placement & Deduplication

Candidate charging locations are generated geometrically from the road network, then filtered for co-location feasibility. This section documents how raw candidates are placed, how true duplicates are removed, and how non-viable remote sites are excluded.

Step 1 — 50 km interval interpolation

For each of the 98 roads with sufficient length (≥40 km), candidate points are placed by linearly interpolating waypoints at fixed 50 km intervals along the road geometry (OSRM-routed from OpenStreetMap). Roads shorter than 40 km receive one candidate at their midpoint.

Candidate count per road
n_candidates = floor( route_km / 50 )  or 1 if route_km < 50
⚠
Spacing rationale: 50 km provides a safety margin below the AFIR TEN-T maximum gap of 60 km. For non-TEN-T roads the regulatory threshold is 100 km; the 50 km spacing therefore doubles coverage density on national roads. Demand density (not optimised per station) is the next lever for refining placement.
Step 2 — Snapping to nearest road geometry point

Each interpolated candidate is snapped to the closest actual waypoint in the road geometry (within 10 km). This corrects floating-point interpolation errors and ensures all proposed stations lie on a surveyed road segment.

Step 3 — Co-located duplicate removal (1 km radius)

The interpolation runs independently per road. Where two named routes share an identical waypoint — common where an autopista and its parallel national road overlay at interchanges (e.g. A-7 / N-340, N-3 / N-400, N-4 / N-401) — two candidates land at literally the same geographic coordinate. Proposing two stations at one physical point is a data artefact, not a network need.

What is removed: any candidate whose snapped location falls within 1 km of an already-selected candidate on a different route. The 1 km threshold is calibrated to catch exact and near-exact co-location artefacts only — it does not remove stations on genuinely parallel corridors (e.g. A-2 and N-2 at 5 km separation serve distinct traffic streams and are both retained).

Greedy deduplication — priority order
1. TEN-T routes  (AP-7, A-7, A-2, A-4, A-6, A-8, AP-1, AP-68, A-3, A-62, AP-71)
2. A- highways  (non-TEN-T)
3. AP- toll roads
4. N- national roads

Within each priority tier, candidates are sorted alphabetically by route name. The algorithm processes candidates in priority order; a candidate is accepted only if it is ≥1 km from every already-accepted candidate. When two routes collide, the higher-priority route retains the slot — TEN-T coverage is never sacrificed to a parallel national road.

ℹ
Effect: In the April 20 run, deduplication reduced 627 raw candidates to approximately 600, removing ~27 true co-located artefacts. Parallel corridors such as A-2 / N-2 (Madrid–Zaragoza) and A-7 / N-340 (Mediterranean coast) are fully preserved with independent stations.
Step 4 — Partner proximity filter

DC fast-charging hubs require an existing venue for co-location: customer amenities (toilets, food, rest), existing footfall, and a grid connection point that can be shared. A proposed station with no fuel station or rest area within 10 km is unviable under this model.

Candidates with no partner site within 10 km are removed from the proposed list and shown as a separate "No-Partner Removed" layer in the dashboard. This is transparent — judges can see which locations were excluded and why.

Partner source
partner_sites = 13,958 OpenStreetMap fuel stations + rest areas near Spanish road network
⚠
Threshold assumption: 10 km is a practical co-location radius for interurban sites — drivers will deviate up to ~5 km from a motorway exit. Sites beyond 10 km from any partner are classified as "greenfield remote" and excluded; they may become viable if new rest areas open or if Iberdrola develops standalone hubs, but that is outside the 2027 planning horizon.

Demand Calculation

For each proposed station, daily charging sessions are estimated bottom-up: fleet size × traffic share × charge probability, adjusted by day-of-week patterns.

Step 1 — Traffic share per route

Source: MITMA (Ministerio de Transportes) interurban traffic segment data 2023–2024. Each MITMA segment is assigned a road identifier (A-7, N-340, etc.). The number of segments per road is used as a proxy for relative traffic volume.

Traffic volume proxy
traffic_route = segments_route × 2,234 vehicles/segment
share_route = traffic_route / Σ traffic_all
⚠
Calibration assumption: 2,234 vehicles/segment is calibrated so AP-7 (5,513 segments) yields ≈12.3M annual vehicles — consistent with DGT toll counts. This is a route-level order-of-magnitude proxy, not actual traffic flow data.
Step 2 — Route base demand
Daily EV charging sessions on route (route-level)
base_route = fleet_2027 × share_route / 7 × 0.40
fleet × route share × weekly÷daily × charge probability

The ÷7 distributes the annual fleet across days: we assume each EV makes approximately one interurban trip per week on average.

Step 3 — Charge probability: 40% corridor / 4% station

The 40% figure represents the probability that an EV on a given highway corridor will need to charge during that trip — based on average range (230 km), trip length distribution, and battery state assumptions from ACEA/IEA survey data.

With approximately 10 stations per corridor on average, the effective probability of stopping at any specific station is ~4%. Both framings produce identical demand outputs — the 40% is applied at the route level before dividing by station count.

ℹ
Why 40% and not 4%? The 40% at route level and 4% at station level are mathematically equivalent when demand is divided across all stations on a route. The route-level model was implemented first; a station-level 4% model yields the same result and is conceptually simpler for single-station analysis.
Step 4 — Day-of-week adjustment

Route-level day-of-week patterns are loaded from route_day_of_week.json (MITMA). The raw file contains absolute traffic volumes, which are normalized to multipliers (÷ weekly average) before applying. This was a critical bug fix — using raw volumes as multipliers inflated P80 by ~3 orders of magnitude.

DOW normalization → 7 daily session values
factor_d = raw_volume_d / avg(raw_volume)
sessions_d = base_route × factor_d
Default factors (no DOW data): Mon 0.87 · Tue 0.90 · Wed 0.92
Thu 0.97 · Fri 1.13 · Sat 1.06 · Sun 1.04
Step 5 — P80 design day & average demand
Design day metrics from 7 DOW values
P80_route = 80th percentile of [sessions_Mon … sessions_Sun]
avg_route = mean of [sessions_Mon … sessions_Sun]

P80 is the design day — we size infrastructure for the 80th percentile demand day (typically Friday or Saturday on highway corridors). Average demand is also stored for context; it is lower (~87% of P80 on most routes).

Step 6 — Per-station allocation

Total route demand is split equally across all stations on that route.

Station-level demand
daily_sessions_p80_station = P80_route / n_stations_on_route
daily_sessions_avg_station = avg_route / n_stations_on_route
⚠
Equal-split assumption: Demand is distributed uniformly across stations on a route. In reality, urban-adjacent stations see higher demand. A route-position weight (e.g. population density proximity) would refine this in future iterations.
03

Charger Sizing

Each station's charger count is the maximum of two independent methods (ensuring we meet both daily capacity and peak-hour service levels), subject to a floor and a grid cap.

Physical constant: sessions per charger
Charger daily throughput capacity
energy_per_stop = 210 km × 0.1775 kWh/km = 37.3 kWh
charge_time = 37.3 / 150 kW = 0.249 h (≈ 15 min)
sessions_per_charger_day = 14 h / 0.249 h × 0.60 = 33.7 sessions
14h = operational window 06:00–20:00 · 60% = availability/efficiency factor
ParameterValueBasis
Average trip segment210 kmMITMA interurban trip distribution
EV consumption0.1775 kWh/kmWLTP average for segment mix (BEV+PHEV)
Charger power150 kWFixed per datathon rules (DC fast)
Operational hours14 h/day06:00–20:00 highway service window
Efficiency factor60%Accounts for connector downtime, idle gaps, non-charging stays
Method 1 — P80 day throughput

Size the station so that chargers can handle the full P80 day's total session volume across 14 operational hours.

P80-day charger count
chargers_p80 = ⌈ daily_sessions_p80 / 33.7 ⌉
Method 2 — Peak-hour 90% utilization target

Highway charging demand is not uniformly distributed across the day — midday (11:00–13:00) and early evening peaks can carry 10–14% of daily sessions in a single hour. We size chargers so that this peak hour reaches at most 90% utilization. Above 95% means cars are actively turned away.

Peak-hour charger count
peak_hour_demand = daily_sessions_p80 × 0.12
sessions_per_charger_hour = 1 / 0.249 h = 4.02 (physical max, no efficiency derate)
chargers_peak = ⌈ peak_hour_demand / (4.02 × 0.90) ⌉
⚠
Peak hour factor (12%): Derived from MITMA highway midday traffic concentration data. In the absence of intra-day charging session data, this is an estimate. Range observed in literature: 10–15%. At 12%, peak sessions require slightly more chargers than the P80-day method on most routes.
Final charger recommendation
Proposed charger count per station
n_chargers = min(12, max(4, max(chargers_p80, chargers_peak)))
BoundValueRationale
Floor (minimum)4 chargersMinimum viable station: economics of grid connection, civil works, and operations only justify ≥4 chargers. Below this, cost-per-session becomes unviable. Also provides redundancy — 1 charger always available if 3 others occupied or under maintenance.
Ceiling (maximum)12 chargersFull hub = 12 × 150 kW = 1.8 MW, which is the Sufficient grid threshold. Beyond 12, a new grid connection is required — treated as a separate infrastructure project. Consistent with AFIR "large hub" specification.
Grid cap (Moderate only)grid_mw × 1000 / 150For Moderate-grid stations, charger count is capped at what the available MW can physically support. For Congested stations, the full demand count is proposed (grid upgrade is flagged separately).
Scenario variants
Conservative / Baseline / Aggressive charger counts
chargers_cons = max(4, ⌊ n_chargers × 0.8 ⌋)
chargers_base = n_chargers
chargers_aggr = min(12, ⌈ n_chargers × 1.2 ⌉)
04

Utilization

Two utilization metrics are reported per station: daily utilization (sessions vs daily capacity) and peak-hour utilization (the critical service constraint).

Daily utilization
Daily utilization %
utilization_pct = daily_sessions_p80 / (n_chargers × 33.7) × 100

Measures how much of the station's total daily capacity is consumed on the P80 design day. This will be lower than peak-hour utilization because demand is not uniformly distributed across the day.

Peak-hour utilization
Peak-hour utilization %
util_peak_hour_pct = daily_sessions_p80 × 0.12 / (n_chargers × 4.02) × 100

Measures fraction of physical throughput capacity consumed during the peak hour. Because charger sizing targets exactly 90% peak-hour utilization, most stations will land near 85–92% on this metric.

ℹ
i-DE coordinate conversion: The XLSX uses UTM Zone 30N (EPSG:25830) coordinates with comma decimal separators. These are converted to WGS84 using a manual inverse UTM formula (no external library). Accuracy: ±1m, validated against known substation locations in Madrid.
Threshold derivation

Thresholds are derived directly from the 150 kW charger standard. Each charger requires 150 kW of firm grid capacity.

Grid status thresholds
Sufficient : grid_mw ≥ 1.8 MW → supports 12 chargers (full hub, no upgrade)
Moderate : 0.6 ≤ grid_mw < 1.8 MW → supports 4–11 chargers (constrained)
Congested : grid_mw < 0.6 MW → below min viable (grid upgrade required)

Note: the bimodal distribution of MW values (most substations cluster at 0 MW or <0.3 MW, almost nothing in between) means the 0.6 MW Moderate threshold changes very few classifications — the majority of non-zero sites are either clearly Sufficient or clearly Congested.

Data quality flag

62% of source records have zero or null MW capacity — a known data pipeline gap in the distributor's open-data feed, not necessarily indicating real grid congestion.

data_quality classification
data_quality = 'measured' if grid_mw > 0 (real substation data available)
data_quality = 'unknown' if grid_mw = 0 (pipeline gap — default to Congested)
✕
Critical caveat: ~299 stations are classified as Congested solely because the nearest substation returned zero MW. These are not verified congestion points — they are data gaps. The dashboard's "Data Gap" KPI exposes this count. True measured Congested = ~88 stations. Headline congestion rate should be read with this caveat.
Assignment method

Each candidate station is matched to the nearest substation within a 100 km search radius using Haversine distance. The substation's Capacidad firme disponible (MW) (firm available capacity) is used directly.

⚠
Distance assumption: "Nearest substation" is a geographic proxy. The actual grid connection point may be a different substation, especially in complex urban grid topologies. Real grid assignments require distributor engineering assessment (Estudios de Acceso). The dashboard popup surfaces headroom and flags upgrade requirements as a starting point for this conversation.

AFIR / TEN-T Compliance

EU Regulation 2023/1804 (AFIR — Alternative Fuels Infrastructure Regulation) mandates minimum fast-charging coverage on the Trans-European Transport Network (TEN-T).

Spacing requirements
Network typeMax gapMin powerDeadline
TEN-T Core60 km≥ 400 kW (2030)2025: ≥ 100 kW every 60 km
TEN-T Comprehensive60 km≥ 100 kW2027
Non-TEN-T national roads100 km≥ 100 kW2030 (recommended)
TEN-T routes in our network

Routes tagged as TEN-T: AP-7, A-7, A-2, A-4, A-6, A-8, AP-1, AP-68, A-3, A-62, AP-71. These carry 147 of the 627 proposed stations.

AFIR gap detection
For each consecutive station pair on a route:
gap_km = Haversine(station_i, station_{i+1})
threshold = 60 km if TEN-T else 100 km
Flag if gap_km > threshold

Result: 58 gaps flagged across 21 routes (6 TEN-T corridors). Largest: A-2 (9 gaps), A-8 (7 gaps), A-7 (5 gaps). These appear as orange circles on the map's AFIR layer.

⚠
Gap detection limitation: Stations are sorted by latitude before gap checking — an approximation for route ordering. On non-meridional routes (e.g. A-8 running east-west) this may slightly misorder stations near route bends. A proper route-following distance calculation would require the full OSRM waypoint sequence.
✕
Existing stations not counted: AFIR gaps are detected only between proposed stations. Existing EV chargers already on the corridor (4,801 nationwide) may already close some of these gaps. A combined existing + proposed analysis would reduce the gap count.
