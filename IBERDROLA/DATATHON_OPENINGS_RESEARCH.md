# Datathon Winning Openings — Deep Research

## What Winners Do Differently

### 1. THE OPENING hook

**The Pattern: 3-5 seconds of silence → ONE bold statement → Pause → Transition**

Winners DON'T:
- Start with "Hello, we're Team X, and we're here to talk about..."
- Thank everyone or introduce team upfront
- Start with background/context

Winners DO:
- Start MIDDLE of the story, not the beginning
- Lead with the gut-punch fact or insight
- Make judges lean forward, not check their phones

---

### 2. MOST EFFECTIVE OPENING TECHNIQUES

#### A. The Stat Attack
> "91.5% of Spain's EV charging stations are congested. We're the team that analyzed why — and where to put the next 568."

**Why it works:** Specific, verifiable, makes judges curious

#### B. The Contrast Opening
> "Spain needs 420,000 chargers by 2030. They have 50,000. The gap isn't a problem — it's an opportunity."

**Why it works:** Reframes the problem as solvable, shows strategic thinking

#### C. The "What If" Opening
> "What if the fastest path to EV adoption isn't more chargers — but smarter ones?"

**Why it works:** Challenges assumptions, memorable

#### D. The Scene-Setter
> "Imagine you're on a highway in Spain. Your EV shows 12% battery. The nearest charger is 80km away. This is why EV sales are stalling."

**Why it works:** Creates visceral connection, judges remember the story

#### E. The Provocateur
> "Building chargers everywhere is the instinct. It's also the mistake."

**Why it works:** Challenges conventional wisdom, makes people think

---

### 3. WINNING STRUCTURE (Assertion-Evidence)

**Slide 1: The Claim (not title slide)**
- One sentence. Bold. Testable.
- Example: "Strategic placement of 568 new stations can increase network capacity 40% without new grid infrastructure."

**Slide 2: The Evidence**
- One chart/visual. Supports the claim directly.
- Judges should be able to say "that's interesting" in 5 seconds

**Slide 3-7: The Proof**
- Methodology (brief)
- Data (key numbers only)
- Results

**Slide 8: The Call**
- One ask. One timeline. Confidence.

---

### 4. WHAT JUDGES ACTUALLY REMEMBER

1. **The FIRST 10 seconds** — if you lose them there, you're done
2. **ONE surprising number** — not 10 numbers, ONE that sticks
3. **Whether you seem to BELIEVE in what you're presenting** — confidence beats perfection
4. **The visual clarity** — can they read it from the back of the room?
5. **Your unique INSIGHT** — what did YOU discover that others wouldn't?

---

### 5. PITCH DECK DESIGN — WHAT WORKS

**Visual Hierarchy:**
- Title: Bold, specific, one line
- Stats: MASSIVE numbers (8-10x normal size)
- Charts: One insight per slide, not data dumps
- Background: Dark works for energy/tech, light for corporate

**Color Psychology:**
- Green (#00974C): Iberdrola = trust, sustainability
- Dark backgrounds: Premium, serious, modern
- Accent colors: Use sparingly for emphasis

**Fonts:**
- Sans-serif for titles (Inter, Arial)
- Nothing under 18pt for body text
- Numbers should be 3x larger than surrounding text

### 8. THE "BUT" TECHNIQUE (from Nature)

Structure: Everyone knows X. BUT the real insight is Y.

> "Everyone knows EVs are the future. BUT the data shows Spain is building the wrong kind of chargers in the wrong places."

---

### 9. JUDGES' INTERNAL THOUGHT PROCESS (what they're thinking during your pitch)

- **0-10 seconds:** "Is this going to be another boring presentation?"
- **10-30 seconds:** "Wait, this is interesting/not interesting"
- **30-60 seconds:** "Do they actually know what they're talking about?"
- **1-2 minutes:** "Should I fund this? Scale this? Partner with this team?"
- **End:** "What's the one thing I remember?"

---

### 10. PRACTICAL TIPS

1. **Rehearse the opening 50x** — the rest takes care of itself
2. **Time yourself** — should be under 90 seconds for intro
3. **Know your ONE number** — if judges remember nothing else
4. **Pause after the hook** — silence is powerful
5. **Make eye contact with judges** — especially after the opening
6. **Hand gestures** — deliberate, not fidgety
7. **Never read slides** — know your content cold

---

### 11. WHAT TO AVOID

- ❌ "Our team analyzed..." — save introductions for end or don't do them
- ❌ Background paragraphs on EVs — assume judges know
- ❌ Thanking organizers first — jump into content
- ❌ Apologizing for anything ("We're students, so...") — own your work
- ❌ Walls of text — one insight per slide
- ❌ Generic claims ("we're passionate about sustainability") — show, don't say
- ❌ Rushing through methodology — summarize, don't detail
- ❌ Ending without a clear ask — always know your next step
