## 🔬 WHAT RESEARCH SAYS ABOUT SCIENTIFIC OPENINGS

### From Nature: How to Tell a Compelling Story

**Key Finding:** "State your main finding in your title, and don't forget to use the word 'but'"

This is the **assertion-evidence structure** used in top scientific presentations:
- Instead of: "Results indicate that X"
- Use: "X happens BECAUSE of Y, BUT most people think Z"

**The "BUT" creates tension.** Tension creates engagement.

---

### From Science Communication Research

**Five principles for opening scientific presentations:**

1. **Be an Entertainer First** (ACS Axial)
   - Before your science can wow them, they have to understand it
   - Before they understand it, you must engage them
   - **Order: Engage → Understand → Wow**

2. **Hook Before Method** (Cornell)
   - "Understand the hook and make them care"
   - Judges have seen 20+ pitches before yours
   - Generic openings = forgotten immediately

3. **Use Emotional Valence** (MIT Research on TED Talks)
   - Positive emotional tone = higher engagement
   - BUT: Don't fake it. Real enthusiasm > polished delivery.
   - Data + emotion = memorable

4. **Start with the Problem's Urgency** (Elsevier)
   - Why does this matter NOW?
   - What's at stake if we don't solve it?
   - Frame the problem before the solution

5. **The Narrative Arc** (URMC)
   - Best presentations tell a story
   - Structure: Setup → Conflict → Resolution
   - Your data is the evidence that resolves the conflict

## 🎯 THE HANS ROSLING METHOD (Gold Standard for Data Presentations)

### Why Hans Rosling's Openings Work

**Three techniques from his TED Talk "The best stats you've ever seen":**

1. **Dramatic Timing** — Pause after the hook stat. Let it sink in.
2. **Visual Revelation** — Show the data BEFORE explaining it. Let them wonder.
3. **Urgency Framing** — "Let me show you something that will shock you."

### How to Apply to Iberdrola Datathon

**The Rosling Opener for EV Charging:**

```
(Silent for 3 seconds while showing a map of Spain with charging points)

"Every dot you see is a charging point. 
Most of them are in the wrong place.

We've been building the wrong places."

(pause 2 seconds)

"Let me show you why we know this — 
and where they should actually go."
```

**Why this is scientifically strong:**
- Visual creates pattern interrupt
- "Wrong place" is a testable hypothesis
- Implies methodology follows ("how we know")
- Creates curiosity without giving everything away

## 📊 THE ASSERTION-EVIDENCE STRUCTURE

**For 10 judges who are technical:**

### Traditional (Weak):
> "Our project uses machine learning to analyze charging patterns."

### Assertion-Evidence (Strong):
> "EV charging infrastructure in Spain is misaligned with actual demand — **BUT** the data to prove this has been sitting unused in Iberdrola's systems."

**The "BUT" signals:**
- This is new knowledge
- There's a misconception to correct
- Your work has real value

## 🎬 SCIENTIFICALLY-RIGOROUS OPENING FRAMEWORK

### Phase 1: The Hook (0:00-0:20)
**Choose ONE of these scientifically-proven approaches:**

#### A. The Mismatch Opener (Recommended for judges)
```
"Spanish EV drivers spend an average of [X] minutes 
searching for available chargers.

Iberdrola has [Y] charging points across Spain. 
But [Z]% of them are underutilized while [W] areas go underserved.

This mismatch isn't random. It's predictable. 
And we've built the prediction model to prove it."
```

#### B. The Cost Ignorance Opener
```
"Every year, [Iberdrola/utility] spends approximately [€X] 
building new charging points.

But we analyzed existing data and found that [Y]% of that 
investment is suboptimal because we're placing chargers 
where demand patterns say they shouldn't go.

Here's what we found."
```

#### C. The Hidden Data Opener
```
"(Show raw data visualization first, silent for 5 seconds)

This is Iberdrola's charging data — [X] terabytes of it. 
You're looking at [Y] hours of charging sessions.

Most people see noise. We saw a pattern. 
Today I'll show you exactly what that pattern means 
for where the next 100 chargers should go."
```

---

### Phase 2: The Bridge (0:20-0:40)

**What judges want to hear:**
1. **What's the problem?** (already established)
2. **What's YOUR approach?** (methodology preview)
3. **What's the payoff?** (impact statement)

```
"To solve this, we used [METHODOLOGY] on [DATA SOURCE]. 
Our analysis shows [KEY FINDING]. 

The result: [QUANTIFIED IMPACT]."
```

---

### Phase 3: The Transition (0:40-1:00)

**Then say:**
```
"Let me show you what we built."
```

**Simpler is better for judges.** They want to see:
- Real methodology
- Real data
- Real results

## 🧠 RESEARCH-BACKED TECHNIQUES TO USE

### 1. The Curiosity Gap
**From psychology:** People hate leaving questions unanswered.

**Use it:**
```
"What if I told you that your grid's existing sensors 
could predict charging demand — 24 hours in advance — 
with [X]% accuracy?

Here's how we did it."
```

### 2. The Stakes Framing
**From American Express research:** Urgency increases engagement.

**Use it:**
```
"Spain has until [YEAR] to meet EU EV infrastructure targets. 
At current placement rates, we'll fall [X]% short.

The difference between meeting and missing that target 
could be [€Z million] in avoided penalties."
```

### 3. The Expertise Signal
**From Northwestern University research:** Establish credibility early.

**Use it:**
```
"We analyzed [X] charging sessions across [Y] locations 
using [METHODOLOGY] — the same approach used in 
[RELEVANT PRECEDENT]."
```

### 4. The Visual Pattern Interrupt
**From TED research:** Showing before telling creates anticipation.

**Do this:**
1. Put up a complex visualization FIRST
2. Don't explain it yet
3. Say: "This looks complicated. It isn't."
4. Walk through it step by step

## 📋 WHAT TO HAVE READY (FOR SCIENTIFIC CREDIBILITY)

### Numbers Judges Will Ask For:
| Data Point | Why It Matters |
|-----------|----------------|
| Sample size (n=X) | "How much data did you use?" |
| Time period | "Is this current?" |
| Methodology name | "What ML/statistical approach?" |
| Accuracy metric | "How good is your model?" |
| P-value or confidence interval | "Is this statistically significant?" |

### Methodology Terms to Know:
- **Time-series analysis** — for demand forecasting
- **Geospatial optimization** — for placement
- **K-means clustering** — for demand pattern identification
- **Gradient boosting / Random Forest** — for prediction accuracy
- **R² or MAE** — for model performance

### Phrases That Signal Scientific Rigor:
- "We validated this using cross-validation on [X]% of the data"
- "The correlation was significant at p < 0.05"
- "Our model achieves [X]% accuracy on held-out test data"
- "The confidence interval suggests [range]"

## ✅ THE SCIENTIFICALLY-GROUNDED OPENING (FINAL VERSION)

```
(3 seconds silence, make eye contact)

"Most EV charging infrastructure decisions 
are made with incomplete information.

Iberdrola collects [X]TB of charging data daily. 
Our analysis shows that [Y]% of charging points 
are placed where demand data says they shouldn't be.

This isn't an opinion. This is what the data proves.

Using [METHODOLOGY] on [X] charging sessions, 
we built a model that identifies optimal placement 
with [Z]% accuracy — and it all fits in a decision tree 
any grid operator can use.

Today, I'll show you exactly where 
the next 100 chargers should go."
```
