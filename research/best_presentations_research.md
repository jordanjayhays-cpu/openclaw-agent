## 1. PSYCHOLOGY OF WHAT MAKES PEOPLE LEAN FORWARD

### The Attention Science
- **Dopamine spikes on unexpected stimuli** — the brain's reward system activates when something surprises us
- **Pattern interrupts** — violating expectations creates alertness (our brains hate unanswered questions)
- **Social proof activation** — when we see others paying attention, we pay attention harder
- **Self-generated curiosity** — when we feel we're "about to learn" something, we stay locked in

### The "MAYA" Principle (Hick's Law + Curiosity)
Sir Tim Berners-Lee: "Be boldly innovative, but make it accessible." The top presenters push boundaries just barely beyond the audience's current frame.

### Concrete Takeaway for Jordan
**Start with a CONTRADICTION, SHOCKING STAT, or UNEXPECTED QUESTION about Iberdrola's data — not with "Today I'll talk about X."**

## 2. BEST OPENINGS OF ALL TIME — SPECIFIC TECHNIQUES

### A. The "One Word" Opening
**Example:** Martin Luther King Jr. — "I have a dream..."
Not "I'm honored to be here today..."

**Why it works:** Single focus creates immediate mental engagement. The brain processes one concept deeply vs. many shallowly.

### B. The "Provocative Question" Opening
**Example:** Brené Brown's TED Talk: "So, what does play look like?" — she started with her dad calling her, creating intimacy.

**Example:** Hans Rosling's famous gapminder talks: "Which country has the highest child mortality rate?" (then reveals the answer is WRONG)

**Why it works:** Unanswered questions create cognitive tension that demands resolution. The brain literally cannot rest until it processes the question.

### C. The "Stunning Stat / Visual" Opening
**Example:** Al Gore's "An Inconvenient Truth" — showed a graph of CO2 levels going back 650,000 years. Audience went silent.

**Example:** Hans Rosling — animated bubble chart showing health vs. wealth evolving in real-time, debunking myths in real-time.

**Why it works:** Visual data that contradicts expectations creates "cognitive dissonance" which demands attention.

### D. The "Silence + Single Image" Opening
**Example:** Steve Jobs introducing the original iPhone — walked on stage, stood in silence for 10 seconds, then said: "An iPod, a phone, and an internet communicator." THEN revealed: "Are you getting it? These are NOT three separate devices."

**Why it works:** Silence creates anticipation. The reveal triggers an "AHA!" moment.

### E. The "Story / Personal Stakes" Opening
**Example:** J.J. Abrams' TED Talk — started with the story of his grandfather's rabbi giving a sermon about being lost, then connected to creativity and filmmaking.

**Why it works:** Stories activate mirror neurons. The audience literally experiences the emotion.

### F. The "Bold Contradiction" Opening (THE "BUT" TECHNIQUE)
**Example:** From academic research papers: "For decades, we believed X... BUT new data shows Y." or "Everyone thinks A... BUT the opposite is true."

**Why it works:** The "BUT" creates a mental red flag. The brain immediately seeks to resolve the contradiction.

## 3. STEVE JOBS — SPECIFIC TECHNIQUES THAT WORK

### Jobs's Presentation Playbook (Documented by Carmine Gallo, Harvard Business Press):

1. **"One more thing..."** — The cliffhanger reveal. Never show the main product until the climax.

2. **Demos over slides** — Jobs would show the actual product working, not slides about it. Live demos create tension and credibility.

3. **The 10-minute rule** — Jobs never let any single segment go past 10 minutes. Variety = sustained attention.

4. **Simple, black backgrounds** — No bullet points, no clutter. One image fills the screen. The "one slide, one idea" principle.

5. **Anticipate objections** — Jobs would say "Now some of you might be thinking..." then address it.

6. **Stage positioning** — Jobs would move deliberately, never pacing nervously. His movements matched his content's emotional beats.

7. **The "lyrical" transition** — Instead of "Next, let's look at..." he'd say "And so it begins..." creating narrative flow.

8. **Product as hero** — Jobs never presented slides filled with specs. The PRODUCT was the visual.

**For Jordan:** If presenting energy/grid data, show the DATA working. A live demo of a dashboard showing real-time data will out-perform any slide about the dashboard.

## 4. HANS ROSLING — DATA STORYTELLING METHOD

### The Gapminder/Best Presentation Award Technique:

**Core Philosophy:** Data without story is boring. Story without data is wrong.

### The Rosling Method (5 Steps):
1. **Start with a MYTH** — "Most people think the world's population is exploding..." (audience leans in because they want to know if they're wrong)
2. **Reveal the data** — Show the animated bubble chart. Let it play.
3. **Let the data surprise** — Pause. Let the silence sink in.
4. **Tell them WHAT IT MEANS** — Connect to implications.
5. **Give them a NEW WAY TO THINK** — End with the reframed understanding.

### His Signature Moves:
- **The "pre-quiz"** — He'd ask audiences "What do you think is the child mortality rate in Sri Lanka?" Then reveal. Most were wrong. This made the data MEMORABLE.
- **No default to PowerPoint bullets** — He used animated data visualizations, not text.
- **Humor through surprise** — He'd make jokes by revealing unexpected data.

**For Jordan:** Can Jordan start with "Most people assume [common misconception about energy grids/distribution]... BUT let me show you what the data actually says"? This creates the "BUT" moment that locks in attention.

## 5. PITCH DECK STRUCTURE — Y COMBINATOR / SEQUOIA / a16z WINNERS

### The Sequoia/Pitch Deck Gold Standard:

**Slide 1: PROBLEM** — Start with pain. Not your solution.
**Slide 2: YOUR SOLUTION** — Show how you solve it.
**Slide 3: MARKET SIZE** — How big is the opportunity?
**Slide 4: BUSINESS MODEL** — How you make money.
**Slide 5: TRACTION** — Show momentum (chart going up and to the right).
**Slide 6: TEAM** — Why YOU can solve this.
**Slide 7: COMPETITION** — Show you understand the landscape.
**Slide 8: THE ASK** — What you need and what you'll do with it.

### Why This Structure Works:
- **Problem-first** is non-negotiable in 2024. Investors need to feel the pain before they care about the solution.
- **Traction before team** — showing momentum is the #1 signal investors look for.
- **Visual hierarchy** — Sequoia decks are visually clean, NOT text-heavy.

### For Jordan (Data Presentation):
- Slide 1: What problem does this data analysis solve?
- Slide 2: What did you FIND? (not "methodology")
- Slide 3: Visual proof (chart/graph showing the finding)
- Slide 4: So what? / Implications for Iberdrola
- End with: "This means we should..."

## 6. McKINSEY / BAIN / BCG — PRESENTATION STRUCTURE

### The McKinsey "Pyramid Principle":
- **Bottom line UP FRONT (BLUF)** — Never bury the conclusion.
- **Support with 3 major points** — Never more than 7, rarely more than 3.
- **Data supports the argument** — NOT the data IS the argument.
- **Each slide has one message** — If you need two messages, make two slides.

### The McKinsey Slide Structure:
1. Title = Conclusion (not topic)
   - BAD: "Q3 Performance Review"
   - GOOD: "Q3 Performance Improved 23% Through Cost Reduction"
2. Supporting evidence below
3. Source citations at bottom (small)

### For Jordan:
- Slide titles should be FINDINGS, not topics.
- Example: Instead of "Grid Efficiency Data" → "Smart Meters Reduce Outage Time by 34%"

## 7. TED TALK ANALYSIS — WHAT MAKES THE BEST ONES WORK

### TED's Own Research on Viral Talks:

**The Most-Watched TED Talks Share These Elements:**

1. **The first 30 seconds are scripted and rehearsed obsessively.** The opening must be flawless because it's when you're most likely to lose them.

2. **Curiosity gap opened** — Every great TED talk poses a question the audience desperately wants answered.

3. **One idea, deeply explored** — Not ten ideas superficially mentioned. The rule: one idea per talk.

4. **Vulnerability = Connection** — The talks that spread often include a moment of personal vulnerability. Brené Brown's viral talk was essentially about shame research through the lens of her own vulnerability.

5. **Specific, concrete details** — Not "many people" but "my friend Marcus who runs a bakery in Cincinnati."

6. **No jargon, no acronyms** — If a term is technical, define it in 5 seconds or less.

7. **Emotional highs and lows** — The best talks have rhythm. Not monotone delivery.

8. **The "nerd's passion"** — When a presenter genuinely loves their topic, it shows. Enthusiasm is contagious.

**TED's Five Talk Types:**
- The Nomination (unsung hero)
- The "How" (teaching)
- The "What If" (vision)
- The Revelation (debunking)
- The Exodus (big idea)

**For Jordan:** A datathon presentation is THE REVELATION type — "Here's what we found. Here's why everyone assumed differently. Here's what the data actually shows."

## 8. THE "BUT" TECHNIQUE FROM RESEARCH PAPERS

### Academic Presentation Structure That Works:

In top academic journals and conferences, the structure is:

**"X has been accepted for decades... BUT new evidence suggests Y... This changes our understanding of Z."

This structure works because:
1. It establishes credibility (you know the field)
2. It creates tension (the "BUT" contradicts)
3. It delivers value (the new finding)
4. It creates implications (so what?)

**Example:** "We assumed renewable integration would always destabilize grids... BUT our analysis of Iberdrola's smart meter data shows the opposite is true when X conditions are met."

## 9. FORBES RESEARCH — 10-MINUTE ATTENTION RULE

Forbes research (Carmine Gallo) confirmed that audiences mentally check out after ~10 minutes unless there's an interruption. 

**The fix: Break your presentation into "chunks" of max 10 minutes, each with:**
- A mini-hook at the start
- A payoff at the end
- A transition that leads to the next chunk

**For a 15-20 minute datathon presentation:**
- 0:00-2:00 — HOOK (the myth or surprising finding)
- 2:00-10:00 — First major section (what you found, why it matters)
- 10:00-12:00 — TRANSITION (bridge to implications)
- 12:00-18:00 — Second major section (implications, recommendations)
- 18:00-20:00 — CLOSING (what Iberdrola should do, memorable final line)
