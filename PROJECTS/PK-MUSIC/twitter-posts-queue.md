## Post 2 (Long)
The Stoics get misrepresented as emotionless robots.

Marcus Aurelius wept. Seneca wrote letters about grief. Epictetus was a slave.

The point was never to suppress feeling. It was to not be ruled by it.

You can feel everything and still act from reason. That's the work. That's the whole game.

## Post 6 (Long)
Existentialism is not pessimism.

When Nietzsche said "God is dead" he wasn't mourning. He was declaring independence.

The void doesn't swallow you. It frees you. No predetermined meaning means you get to choose. That's terrifying AND exhilarating.

Most people can't handle that freedom so they invent new gods to submit to. Money. Status. Algorithms.

Don't.

## Post 8 (Long)
Seneca wrote "We suffer more in imagination than in reality."

This is empirically true. Anxiety is anticipatory suffering. The event itself? Usually survivable.

But we've optimized our environments to maximize anticipation. Endless scrolling. Endless comparison. We live in a permanent state of tomorrow's disaster.

The Stoic answer: focus on what's in your control RIGHT NOW. Nothing else is real.

## Post 12 (Long)
Amor Fati is misunderstood as passive resignation.

It's not. It's the opposite.

When you truly love your fate—every element of it—you stop fighting reality. And when you stop fighting reality, you redirect all that wasted energy into effective action.

The boxer who accepts the fight can finally fight. The entrepreneur who accepts failure can finally try.

## Post 13 (Short)
**Cogito ergo sum is broken.**

"I think therefore I am" assumes thinking = selfhood.

But thoughts arise unbidden. You don't choose them. So who is the "I" doing the thinking?

Existentialists spent a century working through this collapse. The answer they landed on: you are what you do. Not what you think.

## Post 14 (Long)
Marcus Aurelius wrote Meditations as private therapy. He never intended publication.

He was the most powerful man in the world and his main concern was not being corrupted by it.

Every morning he reminded himself: you will die, you are small, power is fleeting, virtue is all that remains.

This man held an empire together through sheer intellectual discipline while the world burned.

What excuse do you have?

## Post 18 (Long)
The absurd, as Camus described it, is the tension between our desire for meaning and the universe's silence.

Most people resolve this tension through distraction. Work. Drink. Scroll. Religion. Ideology. Anything to avoid the void.

But you can also stare directly into the absurd and laugh. That's what Sisyphus does. He embraces the futility AND finds it sufficient.

That's not defeat. That's mastery.

## Post 20 (Long)
Final thought on the Stoic Virtues:

- **Wisdom**: See clearly. Don't lie to yourself.
- **Courage**: Act despite fear.
- **Justice**: Treat people fairly, especially when costly.
- **Temperance**: Say no. Restrain yourself. Build capacity.

These aren't romantic ideals. They're operational principles for building a life that functions when everything goes wrong.

Because it will go wrong. The question is what you've built inside yourself to survive it.

Build accordingly.
